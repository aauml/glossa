# Spanish translation conventions

The Spanish version is not a translation of the English version — it's a parallel article. Same structure, same exhibits, same editorial voice in Iberian Spanish. Translate the meaning and the register, not the words.

## Iberian register, not Latin American

Lecturas uses Spanish as written in Spain, doctoral/editorial register. This means:

- **«» for primary quotes**, "" only for secondary quotes inside «». Never use Spanish double-tilde quotes.
- **No vosotros** in editorial voice (it's an essayistic register, mostly third person and impersonal forms). When the source uses second-person plural and you need to translate it, "ustedes" is a fallback but usually you can rephrase impersonally.
- **Verb forms** that signal Spain: "ordenador" not "computadora," "móvil" not "celular," "aparcar" not "estacionar," "coche" not "carro." But these are rarely relevant in geopolitical pieces.
- **Past tenses**: prefer pretérito perfecto compuesto ("ha dicho," "ha terminado") for events still relevant to the present, pretérito indefinido ("dijo," "terminó") for closed actions. Latin American Spanish tends to use indefinido for both; don't.
- **Dequeísmo and queísmo**: avoid both. "Pienso que" not "pienso de que." "Me alegro de que" not "me alegro que."

## Track-specific register

All tracks share the magazine-essay model (*El País Semanal*, *Granta*, *Jot Down*). But different tracks operate at different points on the accessible-to-specialist spectrum. The key rule: **technical vocabulary is fine; bureaucratic register is not.** A piece can use the word "impugnación" (precise, necessary) without lapsing into "progenitores" (stiff, unnecessary when "padres" exists).

Track-specific guidance:

- **general**: everyday editorial register. Avoid specialist jargon unless the source introduces it, in which case gloss it.
- **ai-policy**: regulatory vocabulary is expected — "tratamiento automatizado," "interoperabilidad," "evaluación de impacto." But the prose around it should read like *El País*, not like the BOE. "Quienes toman las decisiones" rather than "decisores." "Los padres" rather than "los progenitores." "Aplicar" rather than "implementar" (unless you're quoting a regulation).
- **thesis**: academic register is fine — impersonal voice, subjunctive where correct, formal connectives. But academic does not mean dead. "Se ha argumentado que" is fine; a paragraph-long passive construction is not. Keep sentences alive even in formal mode.
- **finance**: quantitative precision is key. Use the correct number-formatting conventions (below). The prose can be crisp and direct — *Papeles de Economía Española*, not a bank report.
- **geopolitics**: historically grounded, careful with partisan framing. Can be more literary than other tracks. Proper names matter: check transliterations.

## Bureaucratic calques to avoid

Spanish has a bureaucratic-legal register that seeps into editorial writing through EU translations, legal documents, and institutional press releases. Lecturas is not that register. Some common traps:

| Bureaucratic calque | Natural editorial alternative |
|---------------------|-------------------------------|
| decisores           | quienes deciden / los responsables de la decisión |
| progenitores        | padres / padres y madres      |
| implementar         | aplicar / poner en marcha / llevar a cabo |
| visibilizar         | mostrar / hacer visible / sacar a la luz |
| transaccionar       | negociar / realizar transacciones |
| posicionarse        | pronunciarse / tomar posición |
| accionar            | actuar / emprender acciones   |
| operativizar        | poner en práctica / hacer operativo |
| interlocutor válido | interlocutor (without "válido" — it's always implied) |
| en base a           | sobre la base de / con arreglo a / a partir de |
| a nivel de          | en el plano de / en (just "en") |
| temática            | tema / asunto / cuestión      |

The test: would an *El País* columnist write this word in an essay? If the answer is "only in a direct quote from an EU regulation," rephrase.

## The billion problem (this is non-negotiable)

English and Spanish use different long-scale vs. short-scale conventions for large numbers. Get this wrong and the article becomes misinformation.

| English      | Spanish                                  |
|--------------|------------------------------------------|
| 1 thousand   | mil                                      |
| 1 million    | un millón                                |
| 1 **billion** (10⁹)  | **mil millones** (NOT "un billón")  |
| 1 trillion (10¹²)    | **un billón** (NOT "un trillón")    |
| 1 quadrillion (10¹⁵) | mil billones                        |

Concrete examples from existing pieces:
- "$5 trillion in deficit" → "5 billones de dólares de déficit"
- "$160 billion in interest" → "160.000 millones de dólares en intereses"
- "$13 billion super-carrier" → "portaaviones de 13.000 millones de dólares"
- "the world's $7 trillion deficit" → "el déficit mundial de 7 billones de dólares"

When in doubt, expand into thousands of millions: "160.000 millones" reads cleanly and removes ambiguity.

## Number formatting

Spanish uses periods for thousands separators and commas for decimals — the opposite of English.

- English: `13,000,000.50` (thirteen million)
- Spanish: `13.000.000,50` (the same number)

In editorial Spanish, very large round numbers usually get written out partially:
- "13.000 millones de dólares" (better than "$13.000.000.000")
- "13 mil millones" is also acceptable but less editorial

For percentages: "20 %" with a non-breaking space, not "20%". In running prose this usually doesn't matter, but `&nbsp;` between number and % is the formal convention.

## Place names that change

| English         | Spanish (Iberian editorial)         |
|-----------------|-------------------------------------|
| Beijing         | Pekín                               |
| Tehran          | Teherán                             |
| Moscow          | Moscú                               |
| Saint Petersburg| San Petersburgo                     |
| Geneva          | Ginebra                             |
| London          | Londres                             |
| New York        | Nueva York                          |
| Equator         | Ecuador                             |
| Strait of Hormuz| estrecho de Ormuz (lowercase)       |
| Persian Gulf    | golfo Pérsico (lowercase "golfo")   |
| Gulf of Oman    | golfo de Omán                       |

Geographic features (`estrecho`, `golfo`, `río`, `mar`) are lowercase in Spanish unless they're at the start of a sentence. The proper-noun part is capitalized. So: "el estrecho de Ormuz," "el golfo Pérsico."

## Names of people

Most names stay as they are in English. Russian names get the Spanish transliteration when one is well established:

- Vladimir Putin → Vladímir Putin (with accent)
- Yevgeny Primakov → Yevgueni Primakov
- Sergei Lavrov → Serguéi Lavrov
- Mikhail Gorbachev → Mijaíl Gorbachov

Chinese names stay in pinyin: Xi Jinping, Wang Yi, Mao Zedong. (Note: in older Spanish texts you may see "Mao Tse-tung" — don't use that, use the modern pinyin form.)

Iranian names: usually transliterated as in English. Araghchi, Khamenei, Ahmadineyad (this last one Spanish does sometimes write as "Ahmadineyad" or "Ahmadinejad"; "Ahmadineyad" is the more domesticated form).

## Date format

Spanish dates run "day of month of year":

- "May 6, 2026" → "6 de mayo de 2026"
- "May 14-15, 2026" → "14 y 15 de mayo de 2026" (or "del 14 al 15 de mayo de 2026" if it's a span)
- "Wednesday, May 6, 2026" → "miércoles, 6 de mayo de 2026" (with comma)

Months are lowercase in Spanish: "mayo," not "Mayo."

## Operation names, project names, technical terms

- **Operation names** in English → keep in English, italicized: "*Operation Epic Fury*" stays as "*Operation Epic Fury*."
- **Acronyms**: most stay in English (NATO, BRICS, SCO/OCS, UN/ONU). Some have established Spanish equivalents:
  - NATO → OTAN
  - UN → ONU
  - SCO → OCS (Organización de Cooperación de Shanghái)
  - UNSC → CSNU (Consejo de Seguridad de las Naciones Unidas)
  - JCPOA → kept; first mention add "(Plan de Acción Integral Conjunto)"
  - WTO → OMC
  - EU → UE
  - BRICS → BRICS (no Spanish equivalent in editorial use)

- **Doctrines and concepts**: usually translated, kept as own term:
  - "axis of resistance" → "eje de la resistencia"
  - "no limits partnership" → "asociación «sin límites»"
  - "civilizational state" → "Estado civilizatorio" (capital E, no quotes)
  - "off-ramp" → "vía de salida" or "salida"
  - "back-channel" → "canal informal" (sometimes "vía no oficial")
  - "regime change" → "cambio de régimen"
  - "toll booth" (in N° 02 sense) → "peaje"
  - "Brussels Effect" → "efecto Bruselas"
  - "regulatory interoperability" → "interoperabilidad regulatoria"

## SVG label translation

Every SVG in a Lecturas piece exists in two language versions (one in `/en/`, one in `/es/`). The structure is identical; only the text differs. When building the Spanish version:

1. Copy the EN SVG markup
2. Replace each `<text>` element's content with the Spanish equivalent
3. Adjust positioning slightly only if Spanish text wraps differently — Spanish is typically 15-20% longer than English, so check that labels don't overlap geometric elements
4. Translate stamp/badge text where it appears (PGA → AGP for "Autoridad del Golfo Pérsico")

## The "you" problem

English editorial voice can use "you" generically ("you can see how this..."). Spanish editorial voice usually avoids this. Recast as:
- "se puede ver cómo..."
- "uno puede ver cómo..." (more colloquial)
- "el lector puede observar cómo..." (more formal)
- Or just rephrase impersonally: "es visible que..."

The impersonal "se" construction is your default tool: "se observa," "se nota," "se ve."

## Articles before country names

Spanish requires definite articles for many country names that English drops them for:

- the United States → Estados Unidos (no article needed in modern usage; "los Estados Unidos" is older/formal)
- the United Kingdom → el Reino Unido
- the European Union → la Unión Europea
- the Soviet Union → la Unión Soviética
- the Netherlands → los Países Bajos
- China → China (no article)
- India → India (no article)
- Iran → Irán (no article needed in editorial use; "el Irán" is archaic)
- Russia → Rusia (no article)

## Verb tense in editorial voice

Spanish editorial Spanish strongly prefers:
- **Pretérito perfecto compuesto** ("ha pasado," "ha dicho") for recent events still relevant
- **Presente histórico** for narrating past events as if happening now (a magazine convention)
- **Indicativo** rather than subjunctive when in doubt — though correct subjunctive use signals high register

Avoid:
- Continuous tenses ("está pasando," "está diciendo") — Spanish uses these less than English. "Pasa," "dice" usually carries the same meaning.
- Excessive use of "estar + gerundio."

## Punctuation differences worth knowing

- **Inverted opening punctuation**: ¿ at start of question, ¡ at start of exclamation. Always.
- **No Oxford comma**: in Spanish, the comma before "y" / "o" is wrong. "Rusia, China e Irán" — no comma before "e".
- **Em dashes**: same usage as English in editorial Spanish, but Spanish also uses long dashes for dialogue (—Hola — dijo). Don't use the dialogue convention; Lecturas uses qa-blocks instead.
- **Spaces around dashes**: Spanish uses spaces. "Rusia — la potencia nuclear que no terminó la guerra fría — sigue..." (En dashes with spaces is standard).

## Self-check before considering Spanish version done

1. Did you translate every label inside every SVG?
2. Did you check every "billion" in the source for the trillion/billón confusion?
3. Are all dates in "DD de MM de YYYY" format?
4. Are place names converted (Pekín, Teherán, Moscú)?
5. Is the lang attribute on `<html>` set to "es"?
6. Is the masthead lang-switch correct (active = ES, link = EN)?
7. Is the callback link pointing to `/es/` not `/en/`?
8. Are the cover pills on both language versions identical (each version offers both languages)?
