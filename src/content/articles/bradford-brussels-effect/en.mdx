<Lede>The article opens with what was, in 2012, a heterodox claim. Europe in the years after the eurozone crisis was widely described as a power in decline: militarily weak, demographically stagnant, monetarily fragile, geopolitically irrelevant. Anu Bradford — then, as now, a professor of law at Columbia — argued that this consensus had missed something essential. Outside the conventional registers of power, the European Union had built an extraordinarily effective mechanism for projecting its preferences onto the rest of the world. It did so without armies, without sanctions, and without the cooperation of other states. It did so through the unilateral promulgation of regulations that the global market had reasons of its own to obey.<Footnote n={1}/> The phenomenon already had a name in domestic American legal scholarship — the California Effect, theorised by David Vogel — but it had not been described systematically as a feature of international regulatory order. Bradford's contribution was to provide that systematic account, to identify the precise conditions under which it operates, and to argue that the EU, in the early twenty-first century, was the only jurisdiction in which all of those conditions converged.</Lede>

<Section number="01" title="What unilateral regulatory <em>globalisation</em> means">

<Standfirst>The conceptual core of Bradford's argument is a distinction. Most scholarship on international regulatory convergence treats convergence as the product of either cooperation or coercion. Bradford identifies a third pathway that operates between the two.</Standfirst>

Political regulatory globalisation occurs when states converge on common standards through negotiated agreements: treaties, conventions, regulatory dialogues, harmonisation regimes administered by international institutions. Coercive regulatory globalisation occurs when one state imposes its standards on another through threats, sanctions, or conditional incentives. Unilateral regulatory globalisation, the phenomenon Bradford names the Brussels Effect, is neither.<Footnote n={2}/> It is the migration of one jurisdiction's standards into another's territory without the former actively imposing them or the latter willingly adopting them. The mechanism is the market itself.

<ContextBox label="What is Anu Bradford's broader project?">
Anu Bradford is a Finnish-American legal scholar at Columbia Law School whose research has reframed the literature on EU regulatory power. The 2012 article being read here gave the concept its name. Her 2020 monograph, *The Brussels Effect: How the European Union Rules the World* (Oxford University Press), extended the framework into a book-length empirical demonstration across data protection, antitrust, environmental policy, food safety, hate speech, and consumer health. Her subsequent work, *Digital Empires* (2023), situates the Brussels Effect within a broader comparative analysis of three competing models of digital governance — American market-driven, Chinese state-driven, and European rights-driven. The 2012 article remains the theoretical foundation on which the later books rest.
</ContextBox>

The intellectual antecedent is Vogel's analysis of the California Effect — the observation that California, by virtue of its market size and preference for strict consumer and environmental standards, was able to set de facto national standards within the United States.<Footnote n={3}/> Manufacturers seeking access to the California market complied with California rules; the scale economies of uniform production then propagated those rules to their entire output. Bradford's move was to recognise that the same logic operates at a global scale, that the EU plays California's role in the international system, and that the literature on regulatory globalisation had failed to account for this. The framework she develops is intended to identify the precise conditions that enable a jurisdiction to exercise this kind of unilateral influence, the mechanism by which it propagates, and the structural limits that constrain it.

</Section>

<Section number="02" title="The five <em>conditions</em>">

<Standfirst>The article advances a five-part theoretical framework. A jurisdiction can globalise its standards unilaterally if, and only if, it satisfies all five conditions. The framework is the analytical heart of the paper.</Standfirst>

The conditions are presented as cumulative. Market power, regulatory capacity, preference for strict rules, predisposition to regulate inelastic targets, and the nondivisibility of standards — each is individually necessary, and they are jointly sufficient.<Footnote n={4}/>

The first condition — market power — is the precondition most discussed in prior literature, and the one Bradford treats with the least originality. A jurisdiction must have a large enough internal market that exclusion from it is costly enough for foreign producers to absorb the adjustment costs of compliance. The EU's roughly 500 million consumers and quarter of world GNP at the time of writing supplied this condition in abundance.<Footnote n={5}/>

The second condition — regulatory capacity — is more analytically distinctive. Market power alone is inert without institutions capable of producing rules, enforcing them against domestic and foreign actors, and imposing sanctions for non-compliance. Bradford argues that this condition disqualifies most large markets. China and other emerging economies may have the consumer base but lack the institutional depth; their regulatory machinery is still being constructed. Among large markets, only the United States and the European Union genuinely satisfy this condition.

The third condition is the political precondition: a domestic preference for strict regulation. In contests between a lax regulator and a strict one over the same conduct, the strict one prevails by definition — its rule binds the firm operating in both markets. Bradford traces the historical reversal that placed the EU rather than the United States in this role. Until the 1980s, U.S. consumer and environmental standards were the strictest in the OECD; by the 2000s, that position had migrated to Brussels.<Footnote n={6}/> The proximate causes she identifies are the European commitment to the precautionary principle, the centrality of the social market economy to EU constitutional identity, and the lower ideological polarisation of European elites compared to their American counterparts.

The fourth condition narrows the regulatory space in which the Brussels Effect can operate. Strict standards globalise only if they regulate targets that cannot escape by moving. Consumers cannot relocate to circumvent product safety rules; capital can. The EU's focus on consumer markets, food safety, chemical regulation, and competition policy reflects this structural constraint — and explains why labour regulation, financial transactions, and corporate taxation are areas where the Effect operates weakly or not at all.

The fifth condition — nondivisibility — is the mechanism through which a regulation that formally applies only inside the EU becomes a regulation that effectively applies everywhere. Bradford distinguishes three forms. Legal nondivisibility occurs when the regulated transaction cannot be partitioned across jurisdictions: a global merger cannot proceed in one market and be blocked in another, so the strictest competition regulator effectively controls all consummated mergers. Technical nondivisibility occurs when the architecture of the regulated activity does not permit jurisdictional segregation: data flows do not know which user is a European citizen, so privacy rules designed for the EU end up applying to all users. Economic nondivisibility occurs when scale economies make dual production uneconomical: a chemical manufacturer that has reformulated its products to meet REACH standards typically finds it cheaper to sell the reformulated product worldwide than to maintain two production processes.

<PullQuote attribution="— Anu Bradford">Market forces are sufficient to create involuntary incentives to adjust to the rules of the strict regulator. In other words, unilateral regulatory globalization entails the dominant jurisdiction imposing an incentive to adjust, followed by reluctant emulation by market participants.</PullQuote>

</Section>

<Section number="03" title="De facto and de jure <em>cascades</em>">

<Standfirst>The Brussels Effect operates in two registers, and the article is careful to distinguish them. The distinction has consequences for how empirical evidence of the phenomenon should be read.</Standfirst>

The de facto Brussels Effect is the primary mechanism. It does not require any formal action by another state. A foreign firm reformulates its products, amends its data architecture, restructures its merger documentation, or alters its supply chain in order to retain access to the EU market. Once that adjustment has been made, the firm — for reasons of scale economy, technical architecture, or legal nondivisibility — applies the new standard to its entire global operation. The EU rule has become the global rule. No legislator outside the EU has formally adopted it. No treaty has been signed. No coercion has occurred. The market has done the work.<Footnote n={7}/>

The de jure Brussels Effect is the secondary mechanism. It emerges when firms that have already adjusted to EU standards lobby their home governments to adopt those same standards, partly to level the playing field against domestic competitors who have not had to incur the adjustment costs, and partly to embed the new equilibrium in law. Civil society organisations frequently amplify this dynamic, treating the EU standard as a normative benchmark and pressing for its formal incorporation into domestic legislation.

The two effects are sequential. De facto adjustment paves the way for de jure adoption — not the reverse. This inverts the implementation problem familiar to international legal scholarship: when a treaty requires firms to change their conduct, compliance is uncertain because the firms have not pre-committed; in the Brussels Effect sequence, formal legal adoption merely codifies a market reality already established.

The article is emphatic that the de jure effect is not necessary for the Brussels Effect to operate. The Effect is essentially about one jurisdiction's capacity to override others — not about its capacity to trigger a formal race to the top. Bradford observes that the literature on the California Effect has tended to focus on the rarer and more visible de jure dimension, and in doing so has underestimated the breadth of the underlying phenomenon. Global commerce, in many domains, already operates under unilateral EU rules even where other jurisdictions formally maintain their own laws.

</Section>

<Section number="04" title="Antitrust, privacy, chemicals, <em>environment</em>">

<Standfirst>The article supports the theoretical framework with four extended case studies. Each is selected to illustrate a distinct form of nondivisibility and to demonstrate how the conditions interact in practice.</Standfirst>

The antitrust case is built around legal nondivisibility. Bradford's principal example is the 2001 Commission decision to block the General Electric–Honeywell merger after the United States Department of Justice had already cleared it.<Footnote n={8}/> The transaction was a forty-two-billion-dollar acquisition between two American companies, but because a merger cannot legally proceed in one jurisdiction and be enjoined in another, the strictest competition regulator's veto becomes the global outcome. The earlier Boeing–McDonnell Douglas case followed the same logic: the Commission did not block the merger but extracted concessions, including the abandonment of exclusive dealing contracts with U.S. carriers, that the Federal Trade Commission had found unobjectionable. Bradford observes that the asymmetry is not one of regulatory capacity — both U.S. and EU agencies enjoy extraterritorial jurisdiction under the effects doctrine — but of regulatory preference. The Commission is simply more willing to intervene, and legal nondivisibility transforms that willingness into global authority.

The privacy case turns on technical nondivisibility. Data flows are intrinsically transnational; the architecture of the internet does not permit a firm to maintain a European privacy policy distinct from its policy elsewhere when the same servers, the same code, and the same data structures serve both populations. Bradford traces this dynamic from the 1995 Data Protection Directive through the negotiation of the Safe Harbor Principles and into the diffusion of EU-style privacy laws to over thirty jurisdictions by the time of writing.<Footnote n={9}/> The case has only deepened since 2012, with the General Data Protection Regulation (entering force in 2018) and the global proliferation of GDPR-style frameworks providing what is now the canonical exemplar of the Brussels Effect.

The chemicals case (REACH) and the environmental case (the Restriction of Hazardous Substances Directive, and later the inclusion of aviation in the EU emissions trading scheme) illustrate economic nondivisibility — the most quantitative of the three forms. The chemical industry is structurally global; reformulating products to meet REACH standards and then maintaining a parallel non-REACH-compliant production line for non-EU markets is rarely cost-rational. The result is that REACH effectively governs a substantial fraction of chemical production worldwide. Bradford documents Dow, Ikea, Lego, Mattel, Revlon, Unilever, and L'Oréal as firms that have applied REACH standards across their global operations.<Footnote n={10}/> She also documents the secondary de jure cascade: California's incorporation of REACH-derived standards into state law, and the adoption of REACH-style frameworks in Canada, Australia, China, South Korea, Japan, and Russia.

</Section>

<Section number="05" title="The limits, internal and <em>external</em>">

<Standfirst>The article devotes substantial attention to the constraints on the Brussels Effect — and the analysis of those constraints is, for a researcher working in regulatory interoperability, perhaps more useful than the affirmative theory.</Standfirst>

External constraints are weak. Markets cannot discipline the Brussels Effect because the EU primarily regulates inelastic targets; consumers cannot relocate to lax jurisdictions in protest. Other states cannot effectively counter the Effect either, since outpacing the EU through still stricter regulation only leaves the counter-regulating state with even less desirable standards than it would otherwise have. International institutions are similarly impotent — the World Trade Organization's jurisdiction does not extend to most of the areas where the Brussels Effect operates (privacy, antitrust, broad swathes of consumer protection), and the WTO's dispute settlement system has, in the cases that have arisen, generally been unable or unwilling to discipline EU regulatory choices that have global effects.<Footnote n={11}/>

The serious constraints, Bradford argues, are internal. They are of two kinds. First, the EU's regulatory authority is unevenly distributed across policy domains; in areas where member states have retained competence (foreign and security policy most obviously, but also direct taxation and significant areas of social policy), the EU as such cannot generate Brussels Effects because it cannot produce binding rules. Second, the political economy of the EU itself imposes limits. As the Effect expands and as more domains come under EU regulatory competence, internal divisions among member states intensify. The expansion of competence and the diffusion of effect are, in the long run, in tension with the political consent that sustains the regulatory machinery in the first place.

This second internal constraint has aged into a more important observation than it may have appeared in 2012. The political economy of EU regulatory expansion — the question of whether member states will continue to accept the costs of stricter common standards as the Brussels Effect comes to define more of global commerce — is now a central tension in the politics of every major EU regulatory initiative, including the Artificial Intelligence Act.

There are also areas, Bradford acknowledges, where the conditions of the Brussels Effect do not hold. Labour standards are divisible: a firm employing workers in Europe must follow European labour rules but can simultaneously employ workers in less protective jurisdictions under quite different terms. Financial transactions are elastic: a financial transactions tax, were the EU to impose one, would simply divert trading activity to non-EU centres. These domains mark the structural edges of the framework.

</Section>

<Section number="06" title="The <em>relevance</em> for AI governance">

<Standfirst>Bradford does not discuss AI governance — the AI Act would not be tabled for another nine years — but the framework she developed in 2012 is now the dominant lens through which scholars and policy-makers analyse the global reach of EU AI regulation. The fit is imperfect, and the imperfections matter.</Standfirst>

The AI Act, in force from 2024, was explicitly designed in the shadow of the Brussels Effect. Its drafters anticipated, and the secondary literature has largely concluded, that the Act will project EU standards onto AI systems deployed globally through the same mechanisms Bradford identified for GDPR.<Footnote n={12}/> A frontier model trained for deployment in the EU must satisfy EU obligations on documentation, transparency, risk assessment, and human oversight; the architectural and economic costs of maintaining a parallel non-EU-compliant version of the same model are, for most providers, prohibitive. The de facto cascade should follow.

Whether it will, however, depends on the precise satisfaction of all five conditions, and the AI case strains several of them. Market power: the EU remains a major market for AI services, but its share of global AI demand is smaller than its share of, say, chemical demand, and the leading frontier developers are concentrated in the United States and increasingly in China. Regulatory capacity: the AI Act's enforcement architecture is novel, distributed between Commission and national authorities, and as yet untested. Preference for strict rules: present, but contested internally — the Act passed with significant disagreement among member states about its stringency, and the political economy of its implementation is genuinely fragile. Inelastic targets: AI services are partly inelastic (consumers do not relocate) but partly elastic (developers can, and increasingly do, withhold products from the EU market rather than comply). Nondivisibility: holds for some systems (foundation models, large language models trained on a single architecture) and not for others (jurisdictionally segmented deployments are technically feasible for many narrower applications).

The literature on regulatory interoperability between the EU AI Act and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework — the principal U.S. soft-law instrument addressing comparable risks — sits squarely on this question. If the Brussels Effect operates fully in the AI domain, interoperability becomes a question of how American institutions accommodate the European standard. If it operates only partially, interoperability becomes a more genuinely bilateral problem of mapping two distinct regulatory architectures onto a shared technological substrate. Bradford's 2012 framework is the analytical instrument with which that question must be asked.

</Section>

<Section number="07" title="Coda">

What remains theoretically robust in Bradford's argument is the five-condition framework itself. Fifteen years of subsequent scholarship has tested it across new domains — financial regulation (where it does not operate), data protection (where it operates almost completely), content moderation (where it operates with significant friction), and now artificial intelligence (where the answer is still being written). The framework has held up across this empirical expansion, and the analytical move that gave the article its lasting importance — the disaggregation of the de facto and de jure mechanisms, and the identification of nondivisibility as the operative conversion factor — has become canonical.

What has aged is the article's implicit normative posture. Bradford in 2012 wrote in a register that was, if not celebratory, at least curious and admiring — describing a form of European power that had been overlooked, was real, and was producing measurable global effects in domains where international institutions had failed. The decade and a half since has complicated that posture. The Brussels Effect is now visible enough to its targets to provoke counter-mobilisation: in the United States, in the form of state-level data-protection laws diverging from GDPR templates and federal legislative resistance to importing EU-style rules; in China, in the form of an explicit Chinese alternative to both American and European digital governance models; in major developing economies, in the form of selective adoption of EU standards combined with active resistance in domains where the political cost is higher than the regulatory benefit.

The reader is left with a question Bradford did not need to ask in 2012 and which the architecture of her framework does not directly answer: what happens to unilateral regulatory globalisation when its targets recognise the mechanism, learn to anticipate it, and begin to build institutions designed to resist it. The conditions she identified remain individually necessary and jointly sufficient. But the political conditions for those conditions to keep holding may be more fragile than they were when the article first appeared. For the doctoral researcher working on EU-US regulatory interoperability in AI, this is the analytical horizon: the Brussels Effect remains the frame, but the frame itself is under pressure.

</Section>
