<Lede>Richard Wolff is eighty-four years old, professor emeritus of economics at the University of Massachusetts Amherst, and one of the most visible Marxian economists working in English. He has been making a version of the same argument for nearly two decades on the weekly show he co-founded, Democracy at Work: that American capitalism is in late-stage decline, that the rest of the world has been quietly preparing for the consequences, and that the United States is governed by people who cannot afford to see what is happening to them. In a recent conversation with Danny Hong, Wolff returns to that frame with the Iran war as his latest evidence. The strait of Hormuz, on his reading, is not an emergency that will pass. It is the moment a long, structural unraveling becomes legible. What follows is what Wolff says, with the figures verified, the assumed concepts explained, and the framework — a declining empire — held in his voice rather than restated as ours.</Lede>

<Callback issue="N° 01" slug="dalio-big-cycle" lang="en">Continues themes from N° 01. Where Ray Dalio reads American decline through the cycles of debt, monetary order, and internal cohesion, Wolff reads it through Marxian class analysis: weakened unions, exported manufacturing, and a state that borrows from oil exporters to avoid taxing its own working class. Two very different traditions converging on similar diagnoses.</Callback>

<Section number="01" title="A <em>particular voice</em>">

<Standfirst>Wolff is not a neutral commentator on global capitalism. He is one of its most consistent left critics, with a fifty-year academic record built inside a Marxian framework. The frame matters before the substance.</Standfirst>

Richard David Wolff was born in 1942 in Youngstown, Ohio, to German Jewish parents who fled Nazism. His father had been a lawyer in Cologne; in the United States he worked as a steelworker. Wolff took a BA in history from Harvard, an MA in economics from Stanford, and a PhD in economics from Yale, where he taught briefly before joining the City College of New York in 1969. In 1973 he moved to UMass Amherst as part of what the department itself called the "radical package" — a cohort of heterodox economists, including Samuel Bowles, Herbert Gintis, and Stephen Resnick, hired specifically to build a Marxian and radical-political-economy program at a major American public university. He retired from full-time teaching in 2008 and now lectures at The New School in New York. With Resnick he developed a distinctive class-analysis framework built around the concept of overdetermination, drawn from Althusser; with Harriet Fraad, the psychotherapist he is married to, he co-founded Democracy at Work in 2012 as a media and educational platform for what he calls "the case for moving beyond capitalism."

He is, on any reading, a serious economist working in a tradition that mainstream American economics has spent the past fifty years marginalizing. He is also a public intellectual with a clear normative project, which is the replacement of capitalism by what he describes as worker-self-directed enterprises, and a clear analytic claim, which is that the contradictions of contemporary capitalism are now too far advanced to manage. The conversation with Hong is a conversation between people who already agree on much of the diagnosis. The work for a careful reader is to bracket where Wolff's diagnostic framing is doing analytical work and where it is doing rhetorical work, and to keep both visible.

The interview itself is, in form, the kind of left podcast appearance Wolff does several times a week. Hong is sympathetic; the pace is loose; the figures come out in conversation, not from a prepared brief. Some of those figures are off, in directions that matter. They are noted in place. The framework — declining empire, structural overreach, capitalism's recurring crisis cycles — is Wolff's longer argument, and is treated here as his.

</Section>

<Section number="02" title="Just in time, just in <em>case</em>">

<Standfirst>The single most useful analytical move in the conversation is Wolff's claim that the past two months mark a shift in how global production thinks about inventory. The watchword, he says, has changed.</Standfirst>

For about forty years, Wolff argues, the trick in manufacturing was called producing *just in time*. The phrase, originally Toyota's, became the operating logic of globalized capitalism: keep inventories near zero, let computerized supply chains pull goods through the system only when an order arrives, treat warehouses as cost. His own book publisher, he notes, no longer prints books and stores them; the physical copy is produced when the order comes in. The model worked because the shipping lanes worked, the chokepoints were notionally protected, and disruptions were the exception that proved the rule.

That model, he says, is gone. The new watchword is *just in case*. The implication runs through the entire architecture of global production: inventories rebuilt, warehouses constructed in places chosen for political risk as much as logistics, supply lines rerouted around shipping routes that can no longer be relied on, railroads and pipelines and internal canals reconceived as alternatives to maritime corridors that may close. "Where you store isn't necessarily where you produce," Wolff says. "That depends on calculations of profitability and probability of interruptions."

<ContextBox label="Background — what was just-in-time?">
The just-in-time production model was developed at Toyota in the 1960s and 1970s under Taiichi Ohno, who treated inventory as a form of waste. The system was exported globally from the 1980s onward as the supply-chain orthodoxy of multinational manufacturing. Two assumptions held it up: that the routes between geographically dispersed production stages were politically secure, and that the cost of small disruptions was always lower than the cost of holding stock. The pandemic stressed the first assumption. The Suez blockage of March 2021 stressed it again. The Iran war, in Wolff's reading, has now broken it.
</ContextBox>

The country he identifies as most prepared for the shift is China. The claim is verifiable in the public record. Beijing began building large strategic and commercial oil reserves around 2018, in part as a deliberate response to the first Trump administration's restrictions on semiconductor exports — a moment, Wolff says, that taught China what *any* category of supply could become if Washington decided so. By December 2025, the U.S. Energy Information Administration estimated China's combined government-held and commercial crude reserves at roughly 1.4 billion barrels. Through early 2026, before the Iran war began on 28 February, China was still adding to those reserves at well over a million barrels a day. The result is a stockpile larger than the equivalent inventories of the United States or Japan, and one that has given Beijing meaningful flexibility as Brent crude has moved into the high $90s.

The asymmetry is now visible in real time. Governments as varied as Japan, South Korea, the Philippines, and India have, by Wolff's account, asked China for access to its reserves to get through the current disruption; Beijing's answer has reportedly been that the reserves were built for China. Whether or not the specific diplomatic exchanges happened exactly as Wolff renders them, the underlying structural fact is hard to dispute: the country that prepared for the world Wolff describes is in a different position from the countries that did not.

</Section>

<Section number="03" title="How the petrodollar was <em>wired</em>">

<Standfirst>Wolff's longer claim is that the deal which made the dollar the world's reserve currency after Bretton Woods was a specific arrangement with Saudi Arabia in the 1970s — and that the Iran war is the event which, in operational terms, has dissolved it.</Standfirst>

The historical sequence Wolff sketches is broadly accurate, though it is delivered in the loose conversational style that compresses a decade of changes into one move. After 1945, the United States emerged as the only major economy not physically destroyed by the world wars. The dollar replaced sterling as the international currency; the Bretton Woods system, agreed in 1944, fixed the dollar to gold and other currencies to the dollar. The arrangement lasted until 1971, when Nixon, facing rising inflation and a run on U.S. gold reserves, unilaterally suspended dollar–gold convertibility. The fixed-rate system collapsed shortly after. The dollar was now a fiat currency floating against other floats. The problem the U.S. then faced was the one Wolff identifies: how to keep the world's appetite for dollars high enough to absorb American deficits without requiring the United States to back them with anything physical.

The solution, gradually worked out over the mid-1970s, was the deal Wolff describes. Saudi Arabia would price oil exclusively in dollars; in exchange the United States would provide security guarantees and a privileged channel for recycling Saudi surpluses into U.S. Treasury securities. The arrangement spread across the Gulf — Qatar, Kuwait, the United Arab Emirates, Bahrain — and effectively across OPEC. Every oil-importing country in the world now needed dollars to buy energy. Every oil-exporting country accumulated dollars it could not productively spend at home, and a large fraction of those dollars flowed back into U.S. government debt. The recycling loop did two things at once: it made the dollar the indispensable settlement layer of global trade, and it gave the U.S. Treasury an unusually deep, unusually price-insensitive class of foreign buyers for its debt.

<ContextBox label="Background — what was Bretton Woods?">
Bretton Woods was the postwar monetary order designed in July 1944 at a conference in New Hampshire. Currencies were pegged to the U.S. dollar; the dollar was pegged to gold at $35 per ounce; the International Monetary Fund and World Bank were created to manage exchange-rate stability and reconstruction lending. The system functioned for roughly 25 years. Its collapse in 1971 — when Nixon ended dollar-gold convertibility — is one of the more under-recognized hinge points in twentieth-century economic history. What Wolff calls the *petrodollar* is the informal successor regime that replaced it.
</ContextBox>

Wolff's strongest line in this section is about Vietnam. The petrodollar system, he says, meant that the United States could fight an expensive and politically unpopular war without taxing the Americans who would otherwise have opposed it. The funding came indirectly, via the Treasury debt that Gulf exporters were buying with their dollar surpluses. Strictly, the chronology is tighter than that — the formal Saudi-dollar arrangements crystallized in 1974, after most of the Vietnam expenditure was already incurred, and the financing of the war was multiply sourced. But the broader claim, that the post-Bretton-Woods dollar order made it possible for the U.S. government to run sustained deficits without the domestic political cost of paying for them through taxation, holds up. It is the structural feature of American economic dominance that Wolff is most interested in, and the one whose unwinding he most wants the reader to see.

The Iran war breaks the deal, Wolff argues, in a way that no previous shock did. Part of the original arrangement was that the United States would station the forces required to keep the Gulf safe for the petrodollar system to function. American military bases in Qatar, Bahrain, the UAE, and Saudi Arabia were not, in this reading, only there for general regional security; they were the security infrastructure of an economic order. The point of the past several months is that Iran has demonstrated those bases are not a source of protection but a source of vulnerability — targets that draw missiles rather than deter them. Qatar reported, after Iranian missile strikes earlier this year, that between a third and a half of its natural gas production capacity had been disabled, with engineering estimates putting reconstruction at five to ten years. Whether or not the specific Qatari figure proves out, the underlying inversion is real: the petrodollar's protection layer has become, in operational terms, a liability.

</Section>

<Section number="04" title="Three kinds of <em>debt</em>">

<Standfirst>Wolff's domestic argument is built around a distinction between productive and unproductive borrowing. The numbers he reaches for are roughly right; some are imprecise in directions worth flagging.</Standfirst>

The framing is the one classical economists use when they want to defend borrowing on its own terms. A loan can be productive: idle savings move from a passive holder to an investor who uses them to produce goods, splits the resulting surplus with the saver as interest, and leaves both parties better off than if the money had stayed in the proverbial mattress. The same legal mechanism can also be unproductive: the borrowed money is used not to expand production but to repay earlier debts, to repurchase shares from existing holders, or to maintain consumption that household income no longer covers. In the unproductive case the loan is a transfer rather than an investment, and the interest is simply the price the borrower pays for postponing a balance-sheet problem.

What Wolff describes as the American problem is that the dominant form of borrowing across all three categories — government, corporate, and household — has shifted decisively toward the unproductive side. Corporations borrow not to build new capacity but to buy back shares, concentrating ownership in the hands of the wealthiest decile that holds the bulk of U.S. equities. Households borrow not to invest in their own productive capacity but to cover wages that no longer reach food, shelter, education, and medical care. The government borrows to cover deficits that are no longer cyclical but structural.

The figures Wolff reaches for in the interview are in the right neighborhood without quite hitting the actual numbers. He puts U.S. public debt at "31 trillion." The most generous reading is that he is using the *debt held by the public* metric, which sits near $31.3 trillion as of early 2026; total gross federal debt is closer to $38.9 trillion. He says the debt "just surpassed GDP at the end of March." This is wrong as stated. Debt held by the public exceeded U.S. GDP during the COVID episode in 2020 and has remained above 100% of GDP ever since; as of the fourth quarter of 2025 the ratio is roughly 122%. The trend is the one Wolff identifies — the bill keeps growing — but the inflection happened five years before the interview.

<InlineNote>On the credit-rating sequence: Wolff says all three major agencies have moved the United States "from AAA to double A." The actual downgrades are by one notch each, to AA+ at S&P (2011) and Fitch (2023), and to Aa1 at Moody's (May 2025). The destination is "AA-plus / Aa1," not "double A." The distinction matters because the next downgrade — the one Wolff is anticipating — would take the rating *into* AA territory proper, which would be a meaningfully larger move than what has happened so far.</InlineNote>

The deeper part of his argument does not depend on hitting the numbers exactly. It depends on the claim that the political economy of repayment is changing. For most of the petrodollar era, foreign holders of U.S. debt were willing to roll their positions at low yields because the United States was the unique safe haven and because Treasury debt was the most liquid financial asset on earth. Wolff's prediction is that this is now eroding from both ends: foreign holders are diversifying out of dollars, slowly but observably, and the domestic political constituency willing to be taxed to service the debt is shrinking as the working class that bears the tax becomes more openly hostile to a government that, on its reading, sends the proceeds upward.

The May 2025 Moody's downgrade is the data point that, in his account, signals where this is going. The agency stripped the United States of the last of its triple-A ratings, projected the federal debt burden to reach 134% of GDP by 2035, and explicitly warned that debt-service costs would crowd out other federal spending. None of that closes Wolff's question. But it is the first time in the modern post-Bretton-Woods order that the three largest rating agencies have all said publicly that they no longer think Washington's fiscal trajectory is investment-grade in the top tier. Wolff is not the only economist treating that as a hinge.

</Section>

<Section number="05" title="An empire that <em>cannot admit it</em>">

<Standfirst>The framework holding the conversation together is the one Wolff has been using for fifteen years. The Iran war does not introduce it. It clarifies it.</Standfirst>

The framework is overreach. Empires, in Wolff's reading and in the longer tradition he draws from, do not collapse in a single event; they spend the late phase of their dominance overcommitting to projects whose costs they no longer can absorb, and they are governed by elites whose careers were built inside the previous phase and who cannot psychologically acknowledge that it has ended. The behavior Wolff identifies in the Trump administration is, in his frame, exactly this. The tariffs that the Supreme Court has now blocked. The sanctions regimes that the targeted countries — Russia, North Korea, Cuba, increasingly Iran — have learned to manage. The military adventures that, in his accounting, do not produce the desired political outcomes.

The line about technology is the one he sharpens most. The United States, he says, committed itself — financially, through its banks and its industrial base — to a vision of warfare in which control of the sky was the decisive variable. Missiles, aircraft, carriers, the long blue-water navy. What the past two decades have revealed is that this architecture is now vulnerable to asymmetric systems an order of magnitude cheaper to build. He overstates the case in the conversation — no American aircraft carrier has been sunk by a drone, and his "fifteen-thousand-dollar drone, ten-billion-dollar carrier" formulation is rhetorical rather than literal — but the underlying inversion in cost-exchange ratios between offense and defense in the contested maritime spaces of the Red Sea, the Persian Gulf, and the Black Sea is real, and it is what makes the Iran war legible as the kind of moment he says it is.

<PullQuote attribution="— Richard Wolff, to Danny Hong">The United States is a declining empire. The only summary I know of that captures this is that one. And a declining empire cannot handle repeated shocks.</PullQuote>

The line is direct, and it is his. It is also the kind of formulation that, on its own terms, only works if you already accept the rest of the framework. The reader who does not — who reads American adjustments to the current shock as costly but recoverable rather than terminal — can hold most of Wolff's specific observations and still reject the umbrella claim. The specific observations, taken in isolation, are mostly defensible: rising debt, declining manufacturing employment, weakening unions, a working class that has been losing real bargaining power for thirty years, a state whose military commitments outrun its productive capacity. The umbrella claim is the one that translates these into a single story of decline. Wolff is committed to that translation. A careful reader can take the parts and weigh the whole separately.

The specific recent behavior Wolff treats as symptomatic is worth naming. Trump's proposed FY2027 defense budget, released in April 2026, asks Congress for roughly $1.5 trillion — a jump from the current $1 trillion baseline that, even using the administration's own numbers, is closer to a 50% increase than the 23% Wolff cites in the interview. The administration has been releasing about 172 million barrels from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve over four months to cushion the oil-price shock, leaving the SPR at roughly 392 million barrels as of 1 May — its lowest level since the mid-1980s, though not, as Wolff says, on track to be exhausted by July. And, in a smaller register, the U.S. military has spent several months conducting extrajudicial strikes on small vessels in the Caribbean and the eastern Pacific identified as drug-trafficking boats. Wolff treats these strikes as the symbolic flailing of an institution that cannot impose the order it wishes it could. The framing is contestable; the strikes are real.

</Section>

<Section number="06" title="Coda — what the conversation is <em>for</em>">

<Standfirst>The framework is Wolff's. The reader's task is to weigh which parts of it travel beyond it.</Standfirst>

What remains uncertain is the timing and the shape. Wolff is right that the global economy is being radically reorganized; he is also right that the petrodollar system, in the precise mid-1970s form he sketches, is not coming back. He is less right about how quickly the rest follows. Reserve currencies have been declared dead before and have not died; the dollar's share of global reserves has been drifting down for a decade without falling off any cliff. The credit-rating sequence is real, but the U.S. continues to borrow at yields that suggest its lenders have not yet priced in the trajectory Wolff is describing. The Chinese growth advantage — Beijing's official 2026 target is around 5% against an American 2% — is also real, but it is narrower than it looks once measured per capita and once China's own structural overhang is included.

What is not uncertain is the inventory shift. The move from *just in time* to *just in case* is happening in the operational decisions of every multinational with exposure to the Gulf, and it will outlast whatever settlement eventually reopens the strait. Whatever else turns out to be true about the Iran war, the supply-chain assumption it has invalidated is the assumption Wolff named first.

A careful reader leaves the conversation with two things. The first is the inventory frame: an unusually clean way of seeing what the past three months have changed in the operating assumptions of global production. The second is a working sense of which parts of Wolff's larger argument depend on the framework — declining empire, structural overreach — and which parts can be held independently of it. Wolff has been making the same argument for two decades. The Iran war is the latest event he has folded into it. Whether the event remakes the framework or the framework remakes the event is the question the careful reader is left to weigh.

</Section>
