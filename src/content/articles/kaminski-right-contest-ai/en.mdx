<Lede>Isabel Castaneda had spent two years of high school calculating. She came from a low-income family and had mapped, course by course, how many college credits she could earn through the International Baccalaureate programme before tuition began. In July 2020, after the IBO canceled spring exams because of the pandemic, an algorithm graded her coursework instead. She failed several IB courses — including high-level Spanish, her native language. The IBO declined to explain its model in any meaningful way, calling it an "awarding model, not a computer-based algorithm," a characterization experts found implausible. Its standard appeals route required fees she could not afford. More than 15,000 students and parents signed a petition. The IBO revised some grades and never explained why any given grade was what it was. This story opens a 2021 Columbia Law Review article by Margot Kaminski and Jennifer Urban — and it is also the article's animating problem: when an algorithm makes a consequential decision about you, what right do you have to push back?</Lede>

<Section number="01" title="Algorithms that fail and decisions you cannot <em>challenge</em>">

<Standfirst>Kaminski and Urban begin not with law but with failure: the accumulated record of AI systems making important decisions badly, and the observation that human decision-making's usual safeguards do not travel well into this domain.</Standfirst>

The IBO grading scandal was not isolated. The same summer, England, Wales, and Northern Ireland used an algorithm to assign university-entrance exam grades to students whose exams had been canceled; nearly 40% received lower grades than teachers had predicted, and officials reversed the results only after public backlash. In the years since, similar controversies have surfaced across welfare benefit systems, criminal sentencing, home health-care allocation, and hiring platforms.

The article catalogs these failures not to build a case against AI in general but to map what is distinctive about machine error. Human decision-makers can be biased, arbitrary, or wrong — this is not new. What is new is the particular character of algorithmic failure. An algorithm makes categorical decisions at speed and scale using rules fixed at the design stage, by people who may not have anticipated every population the system would encounter. This is what Kaminski and Urban call the "long-tail problem." A fraud-detection algorithm for the US SNAP food-stamp programme, trained to flag purchases made in round dollar amounts, flagged Somali-American grocery stores where customers customarily bought meat in whole-dollar amounts. The algorithm was not wrong that round-dollar purchases were unusual; it was wrong about what that unusualness meant in this community. No human reviewer caught it.

Layered on top of categorical error is opacity. For actuarial algorithms — statistically derived and relatively interpretable — a challenger can at least see the factors the system weighed. For black-box machine-learning systems, even the designers often cannot say precisely why any given output was produced. Human reviewers of algorithmic decisions compound the problem through what researchers call automation bias.

<ContextBox label="What is automation bias?">
Automation bias is the tendency of human operators to treat machine outputs as authoritative even when they have reason to question them. Studies in cognitive systems engineering have found that people rely on automated decisions even when they suspect a malfunction. In the context of AI decision-making, this means the human reviewers who are supposed to provide a check on the algorithm may instead defer to it — undercutting the very safeguard their involvement was meant to provide. The EU's 1995 Data Protection Directive was partly motivated by concern over this tendency.
</ContextBox>

A home-care patient whose hours were cut found that neither she nor the caseworker entering her data could "quite understand what was happening." The software vendor implementing the algorithm did not know it had improperly accounted for diabetes — a coding error that had affected hundreds of patients, mostly by reducing their hours. Only individual legal challenges uncovered the problem. Individual contestation is, in this environment, the mechanism by which a long-tail exception surfaces, a systematic error is named, and an adjustment becomes possible.

</Section>

<Section number="02" title="What GDPR Article 22 says — and what it leaves <em>unsaid</em>">

<Standfirst>The GDPR established a contestation right in 2018. The article's first task is to explain it to a US audience that has mostly ignored it, and to show why the most developed version of this right anywhere remains remarkably underspecified.</Standfirst>

<ContextBox label="What is GDPR Article 22?">
Article 22 of the EU General Data Protection Regulation, in force since May 2018, applies when a decision is based solely on automated processing and produces legal effects or similarly significant effects on an individual. Under those conditions, the data controller must implement suitable measures including at least the right to obtain human intervention, to express one's point of view, and to contest the decision. It applies to both public and private entities processing personal data. The right is triggered only for decisions that are solely automated and have significant effects — a scope that is simultaneously narrower and broader than it first appears.
</ContextBox>

What makes this right legally distinctive — and practically slippery — is what the text does not say. It does not define what adequate contestation must consist of, specify timelines, require particular content for the response, or name the substantive grounds on which a challenge can succeed. The regulators' guidance largely restates the GDPR's own language. One example of compliance offered is providing "a link to an appeals process at the point the automated decision is delivered to the data subject, with agreed timescales for the review and a named contact point for any queries." This is a standard that has received minimal rule-like content.

The right has a genealogy. A French data protection law from 1978 already prohibited automated decisions about citizens without a right to "dispute the data and logic used." The EU's 1995 Data Protection Directive contained a precursor provision, Article 15, that Lee Bygrave later described as "all dressed up but nowhere to go" — rarely invoked, poorly understood, easily circumvented. Some Member States implemented Article 15 as a prohibition on automated decision-making; others, like the UK, implemented it as a right to opt out. The opt-out approach put the burden on individuals, few of whom invoked the right. Article 22 improved on the Directive by adding explicit contestation language and by being interpreted as a prohibition (with exceptions) rather than as an opt-out right.

Through most of the GDPR's early years, the contestation right's practical content remained thin. The first judicial recognition of a right to explanation under Article 22 came in a Dutch court in 2021, in cases involving Uber and Ola drivers — the same year this article was published. The right to contest itself had not, as of 2021, been the subject of any known enforcement action. As Emre Bayamlioglu observed, Article 22's contestation right was "strangely both central to the GDPR's system of algorithmic accountability and barely articulated."

</Section>

<Section number="03" title="A right with deep roots and no American <em>address</em>">

<Standfirst>The United States lacks any cross-sectoral right to contest AI decisions. Kaminski and Urban trace this absence back to a narrowing of concerns that were once at the center of American data-privacy thinking — and forward to recent EU developments that have widened the gap.</Standfirst>

<ContextBox label="What was the HEW Report?">
In 1973, the US Department of Health, Education, and Welfare published Records, Computers and the Rights of Citizens, an influential report on automated data processing by both government and private entities. The report is widely considered the foundational document of US data privacy law. Its core principles — known as the Fair Information Practice Principles — became the basis for sectoral privacy laws including HIPAA and COPPA. The report framed automated record-keeping explicitly as a due process problem, calling for individuals to be able to "contest the label as applied to him." It is both a data privacy document and, as Kaminski and Urban argue, a due process document.
</ContextBox>

The HEW Report stands as a counterargument to the idea that dignity and due process in the context of automation are exclusively European concerns. Its reasoning sounds in accuracy ("a serious consequence of putting record keeping in the hands of data-processing specialists is that questions which involve issues of social policy are sometimes treated as if they were nothing more than questions of efficient technique"), in fairness ("the likelihood of unfair or inappropriate decisions about the individual"), and in what the report calls "statistical stereotyping" — using group-level data to predict individual behavior. The way to mitigate these problems, the report noted, is to "permit an individual to know that he has been labelled a risk and to contest the label." This is, in all but name, the right Kaminski and Urban argue for.

The US has since operationalized a watered-down version of these principles through a notice-and-choice model that places the entire burden of privacy protection on individuals who are expected to read disclosures and make informed decisions. As scholars from Daniel Solove to Woodrow Hartzog have documented, this approach creates a legal fiction of consent while leaving individuals effectively powerless. The HEW Report's procedural protections — including the right to contest — were progressively narrowed, not because they were rejected in principle, but because the regulatory model that emerged around them could not sustain them.

<ContextBox label="EU AI Act — Articles 85 and 86 (2024)">
The EU AI Act, adopted in 2024 and entering into force in stages through 2026-2028, adds two contestation-adjacent rights for high-risk AI systems. Article 86 creates a right to explanation when a high-risk system produces a decision with significant effects on health, safety, or fundamental rights — covering how the system was involved, in plain language. Article 85 creates a right to lodge a complaint with national market surveillance authorities. These provisions run alongside the GDPR's Article 22 rather than replacing it. The practical interaction between the two frameworks remains largely unresolved.
</ContextBox>

Meanwhile, the right to contest has gained traction elsewhere. Brazil's data protection law, enacted in 2018, includes a right to request review of AI decisions. The OECD's 2019 recommendations on AI include a right to challenge outcomes. Canada's privacy commissioner recommended in 2020 that Canadian law be revised to include a right to contest, explicitly dropping the GDPR's "solely" qualifier to cover decisions with any significant AI involvement. The Council of Europe's amended data protection convention and its 2020 recommendations on algorithmic systems both call for "effective means to contest relevant determinations and decisions." The United States is increasingly an outlier — not because its tradition rejects these concerns, but because its regulatory architecture has not kept up with them.

</Section>

<Section number="04" title="Accuracy, rule of law, and the dignity argument the US <em>forgot</em>">

<Standfirst>The article's theoretical section makes a case from classical due process theory, moving through three channels — accuracy, rule of law, and dignity — and responding directly to the most prominent scholarly rejection of individual contestation rights.</Standfirst>

The case for individual due process against human decision-makers is old. Extending it to machine decisions requires more argument, and Kaminski and Urban provide it through three channels.

The first is accuracy. Due process catches error before it compounds. Individual challenges reveal what systemic oversight misses — specifically, the long-tail case the algorithm was not designed to handle. The Somali-American grocers' vindication came through individual contestation. The revelation that a home-care algorithm miscounted diabetes came through individual legal action. Critics of contestation rights argue that systemic audits do this job better. Kaminski and Urban respond that audits cannot surface what they do not know to look for.

The second channel is rule of law. A decision-making system that cannot be challenged cannot be held to consistency. Contestation, paired with the reason-giving it requires, disciplines those who make decisions to apply rules evenly and to demonstrate that they have. An algorithm may find that credit risk correlates with sock color. Even if the correlation is statistically backed, it may feel unreasonable — and may be discriminatory if sock color correlates with gender or race. An individual challenge can surface both the irrationality and the discrimination; a system audit may never encounter the case that makes the pattern visible.

<ContextBox label="What is the Mathews v. Eldridge balancing test?">
Mathews v. Eldridge (1976) is the US Supreme Court case that established a three-factor cost-benefit test for determining what process is due in a given context. Courts weigh the private interest at stake, the risk of error under current procedures and the probable value of additional safeguards, and the government's interest in efficiency. Critics of individual due process for AI invoke Mathews to argue that the cost of expert analysis required to challenge an algorithmic system would outweigh the benefits. Kaminski and Urban note, however, that Danielle Citron has argued the cost of correcting one algorithm should be weighed against the benefit that correction provides for all future cases — not just the individual challenger's case.
</ContextBox>

<PullQuote attribution="— Patient advocate, quoted in Kaminski &amp; Urban (2021)">We reduce a person's humanity to a number.</PullQuote>

The third channel is dignity. Much of the US scholarship on algorithmic accountability has dismissed dignitary arguments as vague European imports, or as too "ambiguous and contested" (Huq's phrasing) to ground a legal right. Kaminski and Urban push back. The HEW Report's concerns about automation were dignitary. The French and Portuguese data protection laws of the 1970s — precursors to the GDPR — were partly motivated by concern for human dignity in the face of "data shadows." The Kantian categorical imperative — that individuals must be treated as ends, not means — translates directly: using proxy categories to make decisions about individuals treats them as objects unless they are afforded opportunities for individualized judgment.

The article's main interlocutor is Aziz Huq, who argues that the purpose of individualized due process is essentially utilitarian — ensuring systemic accuracy — and that class actions and ex ante regulation serve this purpose better than individual challenges. He calls instead for a "right to a well-calibrated machine decision." Kaminski and Urban respond that this conflates instrumental and dignitary purposes of process. A right to challenge a decision made about you is not reducible to a calibration tool for the system; it is a form of participation, an acknowledgment that a decision was made about a person rather than a data point. The line from the home-care attorney does not disappear by showing that audits are cheaper.

</Section>

<Section number="05" title="Two axes, four archetypes, and the examples that <em>test</em> them">

<Standfirst>The article's most enduring contribution is a 2x2 typology of contestation designs, mapped along a rules-versus-standards axis and a procedural-versus-substantive axis, then applied to four existing legal regimes to show what each design choice costs.</Standfirst>

<ContextBox label="What is the rules-versus-standards distinction?">
In legal theory, a rule provides clear and precise content ex ante ("do not drive above 75 mph"), while a standard is determined by an interpreter ex post ("drive reasonably"). Rules have the benefit of clarity and lower compliance costs; standards have the benefit of flexibility and avoiding over- or under-inclusiveness. Kaminski and Urban apply this distinction to contestation mechanisms: a contestation rule specifies timelines, formats, and procedures in advance; a contestation standard states that a right to contest exists and leaves the details to whoever implements it.
</ContextBox>

Having established that a contestation right should exist, Kaminski and Urban turn to what it should look like. They cross the rules/standards axis with a second variable: whether the contestation right has substantive or procedural content. A substantive right specifies the grounds on which a challenge can succeed — erroneous data, prohibited discrimination, sensitive data categories. A purely procedural right specifies only how to challenge, not when you win. The combination produces four archetypes.

<ContextBox label="What is DMCA notice-and-takedown?">
Section 512 of the US Digital Millennium Copyright Act (1998) established one of the most widely adopted privatized dispute resolution processes in the digital age. Copyright holders can send takedown notices to online platforms identifying allegedly infringing content. If a platform removes the content expeditiously, it receives safe harbor from secondary copyright liability. Targets can file a counter notification requesting restoration, after which the copyright holder must file a lawsuit within 10-14 days or the material goes back up. In practice, platforms have strong incentives to err on the side of removal, and the counternotice mechanism is barely used.
</ContextBox>

The GDPR's Article 22 falls in the first quadrant: a contestation standard with a procedural focus. It tells companies they must allow challenges but says almost nothing about what those challenges must consist of or when they should succeed. Member States have implemented the right in sharply different directions — the UK went heavily procedural (one-month timelines, written responses required), Slovenia anchored its version to antidiscrimination law, Hungary and France prohibited automated decisions based on sensitive data categories.

The DMCA illustrates the second quadrant: a contestation rule with a procedural focus. The statute specifies in detail what notices and counternotices must contain, how quickly the platform must act, and what happens at each stage. The underlying substance — copyright infringement — exists but is rarely engaged in practice. Empirical research by Urban and others found that 31% of a large sample of takedown notices sent to Google Search were questionable, and 70% of those sent to Google Image Search were fundamentally flawed. Precise process without substantive engagement produces a system where the stronger party dominates and the contestation mechanism becomes dead letter.

The EU's Right to Be Forgotten illustrates the third quadrant: a contestation standard with a substantive focus. The Court of Justice in Google Spain (2014) established a balancing test between individual privacy interests and public interest in access to information, but said almost nothing about procedure. Google built its own review process; regulators gradually elaborated the criteria. The standard has become more rule-like over time through this back-and-forth, though it was never designed that way. Roughly 58% of targeted content stays up, according to Google's transparency reports.

<ContextBox label="What is the Fair Credit Billing Act?">
The Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA, 1974) establishes a US consumer right to contest erroneous credit card charges. The statute defines "billing error" precisely — a charge not made by the cardholder, a charge in the wrong amount, goods not delivered, accounting errors — and specifies detailed timelines for complaints and responses. Credit card companies must investigate and respond within defined periods. The system is free, accessible, and fast, with companies ruling for consumers roughly 80-90% of the time. Consumer advocates broadly regard it as a success, and scholars have pointed to it as a model for digital dispute resolution.
</ContextBox>

The FCBA occupies the fourth quadrant: a contestation rule with a substantive focus. It works partly because "billing error" is easy to define — a specificity that is much harder to achieve for discrimination or bias in AI decision-making. But the FCBA also works because of its incentive structure: investigating a billing dispute is expensive for the credit card company; refunding it is cheap. The statute tips the cost calculation toward resolution rather than denial, without requiring companies to be generous — just making generosity the cheaper option.

</Section>

<Section number="06" title="Process without substance is an empty <em>corridor</em>">

<Standfirst>The article's final section integrates findings from the four case studies to argue that an effective right to contest AI requires three things most privatized contestation schemes fail to provide simultaneously: meaningful notice, genuine reason-giving, and incentive structures that press even nonneutral decision-makers toward fair outcomes.</Standfirst>

The hardest lesson from the case studies is that procedural clarity and substantive grounding are both necessary and neither sufficient. The DMCA provides procedural clarity without substantive engagement. GDPR Article 22 provides a substantive aspiration without procedural clarity. The FCBA provides both — but only because its substantive scope is simple enough to anchor precisely.

Meaningful notice is not a link buried in a privacy policy. It is information sufficient to allow a challenge — specific enough that the individual knows what decision was made, on what basis, and what avenue exists for contesting it. Reason-giving is distinct from transparency about the algorithm's general logic. It is information specific enough to allow the individual to identify what was wrong in their particular case. The GDPR calls this "meaningful information about the logic involved" and pairs it with a "right to explanation" for individual decisions. Member States have interpreted these at widely different levels of specificity. France's public-sector approach — requiring officials to master the algorithmic processing enough to explain, in detail and in intelligible form, how it applied to the specific person — represents the most demanding interpretation.

The article's most pointed observation concerns the decision-maker. A neutral arbiter is core to due process, but privatized contestation schemes almost never include one. Under Article 22, the company that uses AI to decide about a person is also the arbiter of challenges to those decisions. This can be mitigated by structuring incentives. The FCBA shows that even a nonneutral decision-maker — the credit card company — can produce fair outcomes when the system makes accuracy cheaper than error. The DMCA shows what happens when incentives go the other way: platforms remove content reflexively because potential copyright liability dwarfs the costs of wrongful removal.

Kaminski and Urban argue for a hybrid approach for the US. A cross-sectoral floor — a right to contest wherever AI makes decisions with significant effects, regardless of industry — supplemented by sector-specific rules in domains where existing regulatory regimes already have more developed due process traditions: criminal justice, credit, housing, employment. The floor should combine clear substantive rules (an AI decision cannot be made on the basis of racial data or gender), flexible substantive standards (decisions must not be "discriminatory" or "biased," with open lists of examples and regulatory guidance), and clear enough procedural rules that individuals can actually use the right. It should be embedded in a regulatory environment with systemic governance tools — impact assessments, auditing requirements, documentation obligations, whistleblower protections — that complement the individual right rather than substituting for it.

</Section>

The article appeared in 2021, when the EU AI Act was still a commission proposal and Article 22 had not yet seen its first judicial interpretation. Five years on, the EU has added Articles 85 and 86 to the contestation architecture, building an increasingly layered system that pairs individual rights (explanation, complaint, contestation) with systemic governance (conformity assessments, documentation, market surveillance). The United States still has no cross-sectoral contestation right.

What Kaminski and Urban's framework most sharply exposes is the gap between having a contestation right on paper and having one that works. The GDPR's Article 22 has been in force since 2018; the first court to recognize a right to explanation under it did so in 2021. The DMCA's counternotice mechanism has existed since 1998 and is nearly unused. Procedural architecture does not, by itself, produce contestation. It produces the conditions under which contestation becomes possible — conditions that still require substantive grounding, accessible process, and incentive structures that press those who decide toward fair outcomes rather than expedient ones.

The article does not claim that getting these three things right is easy. It claims that not getting them right is already costly, and that the cost is borne by people who have no language for what they have lost except that the number the machine assigned them was wrong.
